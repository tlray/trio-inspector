How the algorithm (oref) works — concepts

The loop cycle: a fresh decision every 5 minutes

On every new sensor reading Trio computes how much insulin is still active (IOB), how many carbs are still absorbing (COB), and where glucose will end up if it does nothing extra. Based on that it picks a temp basal (higher, lower or zero) and possibly a small automatic correction bolus (SMB). Five minutes later everything is recomputed from scratch — every decision is small and revocable.

The four forecast curves (IOB, ZT, COB, UAM)

The algorithm draws several futures: IOB — what happens as the active insulin plays out; ZT (zero-temp) — the worst-case path if the pump delivers nothing from now on; COB — the path including announced carbs still absorbing; UAM — an estimate for an unannounced rise (a meal without carb entry, hormones, stress). Trio doses on the most cautious relevant scenario, not on the current number. Select a moment in the chart to see these curves drawn from that point.

eventualBG — where things land if nothing special happens

The expected glucose once all IOB and COB have played out. If it sits above target, Trio wants more insulin; below target, Trio winds the basal down or to zero — even if you are high right now. That is why you sometimes see a zero temp at 10 mmol/L: according to the model the insulin already on board is enough.

minGuardBG — the hypo guard

The lowest point of the most cautious forecast curve. If it dips under the safety threshold (4.4 mmol/L), Trio blocks all SMBs and sets a zero temp, no matter how high you are. In the log this shows as minGuardBG 3.5<4.4. It is the strongest brake in the system.

SMB and maxBolus

An SMB (super micro bolus) is a small automatic bolus used instead of basal alone — faster, but bounded: per cycle at most a fraction of the computed need (typically 0.4–0.5 U here). If the algorithm wants 1.6 U you still get only one capped SMB per cycle. There is also a minimum interval between SMBs: right after one you will see maxBolus 0. Waiting 2m 30s to microbolus again. Safety over speed: if the forecast turns out too aggressive, as little as possible is already irreversible.

maxSafeBasal — the basal ceiling

A temp basal may never exceed a multiple of your scheduled basal (and your own max-basal setting). If the algorithm wants 3.5 U/h against a 1.4 U/h ceiling the log shows adj. req. rate: 3.55 to maxSafeBasal: 1.4. A structurally low basal schedule therefore also lowers this ceiling — the algorithm can push less hard at night.

Zero temp

Trio’s main defence against lows: basal fully off. It happens pre-emptively as soon as a forecast dips below target or threshold. You will often see it alternate with SMBs: dose → forecast dips → zero temp → forecast recovers → dose again. That pumping rhythm is normal with a strong ISF.

Autosens & ISF — how hard one unit works

ISF is how far one unit of insulin drops you (2.5 mmol/L here). Autosens compares recent hours against what the model expected: if insulin worked weaker than expected the ratio rises above 1 and the effective ISF shrinks (dose more aggressively). In the detail panel this shows as e.g. ISF 2.5→2.1 at ratio 1.2. A hormonal overnight rise (dawn phenomenon) feeds into this only slowly — which is why the algorithm often runs behind it.
